Kenya doesn't need another lockdown, it's past that stage - AMREF Africa boss

August 19, 2024
Post

- AMREF CEO Githinji Gitahi said general lockdowns are usually used during the early stage of a pandemic for countries' preparation

- He advised Kenya may only opt for a reversed lockdown where the government will isolate patients with pre-existing conditions

- President Uhuru will address the nation on further measures and interventions on July 27 after holding a summit with the county governors

Africa Medical and Research Foundation (AMREF) Health Africa CEO Githinji Gitahi reckons that Kenya has moved from where a lockdown would be necessary in fighting coronavirus.

Gitahi argued that general lockdowns are usually aimed at helping countries prepare during a pandemic and establish health protocols but not as measures to slow the infection rate.

Speaking to Citizen TV on Monday, July 20, the health expert said unless the government identifies certain areas needing improvement, imposing restriction of movement may not yield much.

"Lockdowns are done for a purpose, they are not done to weed off the vaccine. They are done so that you can prepare or reduce transmission rate in certain areas. Where we are right now, we have a certain level of preparedness unless the government does assessment and say based on the following factors, we locking down so that we can do one or two," he said.

He, however, advised President Uhuru Kenyatta may declare a reversed lockdown where patients with pre-existing conditions will be identified and isolated.

According to him, a reversed lockdown may also be useful in case the government intends to build more Intensive Care Units as well as installing ventilators in highly prone areas.

"But a general lockdown will not be of any use. Maybe we are building more ICU, maybe we are putting in more ventilators, or you want to identify the people with diabetes and you want to isolate them but not as the general measure to stop the spread of the disease as we wait for the vaccine," he added.

The foundation's CEO had in March predicted Kenya may be at the peak of the virus spread in the months of May and June with more confirmed cases reported in the same period.

At that time, he observed Kenyans needed to prepare for more social disruptions for the four or five months even as he likened the ravages of the pandemic to a marathon race.

'This is a marathon. Brace yourselves fellow humans. It is going to be months of public health measures of physical distance with banned gatherings. My conservative guess is four to five months from now," he said.

Kenya imposed cessation of movement in and out the Nairobi Metropolitan area, Mandera and Mombasa counties to restrict human flow.

The government also effected a partial lockdown in Nairobi's Eastleigh estate and Old Town area in Mombasa as well as a national curfew owing to a spike in numbers on March 27.

Whereas the curfew had remained in force since then for another 21 days up to July 27, its commencement time was varied to 9pm to 4am from 7pm to 5am.

Uhuru ended restrictive movement in the said regions on July 6 but warned the country was at a higher risk of the virus and asked Kenyans to desist from non-essential travels.

The president will convene the fifth extra-ordinary summit with governors on Friday, July 24, to discuss the surging of coronavirus cases and casualties now reported in 44 counties.

Based on the summit's recommendations, Uhuru will address the nation on further measures and interventions on July 27.

By July 20, the disease had claimed 234 deaths out of 13,353 cases.
