Count to Conserve: Gov't Launches First-Ever National Wildlife Census in Kenya

July 05, 2024
Post

- The counting of the wild animals has never been done in the country

- The excise will be launched at Shimba Hill National Reserve and later in other parks

- The counting will give an overall picture of national heritage and boost the ongoing efforts to protect wildlife

Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS) is set to launch a national census for wild animals across the country.

The counting of the wild animals will be launched at Shimbi Hills National Reserve then rolled out in different parks across the country by KWS officers.

The exercise aims to preserve and protect the wild animals faced with different dangers ranging from forest fire to poaching.

The programme will be carried out by Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS) officers across the country at the parks where they are stationed.

"As a part of the efforts towards the preservation and protection of wildlife in Kenya, the Ministry of Tourism and Wildlife will launch its first-ever National Wildlife Census #CountToConserve in collaboration with Kenya Wildlife Service and Wildlife Research and Training Institute," the Service stated.

The census will enable the Ministry of Tourism to know the number of wild animal in the county, which would aid in planning purposes to end the challenges faced by the animals.

TUKO.co.ke had reported that Kenya recorded zero rhino death from poaching in 2020 for the first time in 20 years.

The success was as a result of the fight by KWS against the poaching of rhinos in the country.

Brigadier John Waweru, the director-general of KWS, attributed the incredible record to good leadership and teamwork in the department.

Speaking to the BBC, Wawaru said KWS invested heavily in intelligence-led operations to monitor the rhinos' whereabouts and enhance their maximum safety throughout the year.

"Through my teams, I have enhanced anti-poaching and intelligence-led operations as well as strengthening collaborations with law enforcement agencies and the local communities. We have continuously reviewed policies and strategies to include solutions to emerging issues. Indeed, I am very proud of the team that I work with, and I expect that even this year we can do it again; I don't see the reason why we shouldn't," said Waweru.
