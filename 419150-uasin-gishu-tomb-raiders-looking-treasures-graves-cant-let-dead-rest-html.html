Unlike in the Biblical times, an empty grave does not connote the resurrection of Jesus; in Uasin Gishu, it is a pointer to a serious brand of crime in the area.

Grave robbers have ignored the ‘Rest In Peace’ inscriptions on tombstones and have violated the corpses resting place-making away with valuables.

Invasion of late Huruma ward rep Peter Chomba's grave

After the death of Huruma ward rep Peter Chomba and his burial in October 2020, grave robbers attempted to exhume his remains at the Kamukunji cemetery on the outskirts of Eldoret town.

Their intention clearly was robbing from his corpse expensive suits, shoes, watches, and the coffin.

The criminals were, however, unsuccessful as the grave was sealed off from grave robbers by concrete.

In parts of formerly Kenya’s white highlands, large-scale colonial farmers resided in huge swathes of land dotted with graves.

The most famous was the Commonwealth War Memorial graves.

The thriving black market has recently seen an increase in violation of cemeteries in search of valuable relics that fetch handsome amounts of cash.

Defaced tombstones are scattered through the once beautiful cemetery, with the metallic epitaphs missing due to the lucrative scrap metal business.

Wealthy colonial farmers buried with precious valuables

Popular belief is that wealthy colonial farmers were buried with their worldly possession in fancy graves, giving rise to grave robbery.

The Eldoret Cemetery contains five Commonwealth burials of the Second World War and one French war grave.

One could expect the heroes here to be resting in peace, but that does not seem to be the case since the cemetery is now a shell of its former self.

A spot check by TUKO.co.ke at the cemetery revealed that it is the only grave of Private Marange (number 14503), a French veteran who died on June 26, 1943, and was buried there undergoing constant renovations.

This has been made possible by the financial support from the Directorate of Heritage, Memory and Archives of the Ministry of the Armed Forces and the French Embassy's defence mission.

Graves lie in a dilapidated state

Other graves lie in a state of disarray, and locals are concerned that failure to preserve the open-ended Commonwealth Military Cemetery might lead to its total depletion.

The destruction of the graves has given rise to development by alleged land grabbers who have shifted the signages leading to the graves.

"Construction of residential and commercial buildings is ongoing day and night, the graves will soon be missing, history will be lost," said Samuel Lotodo, a resident of Subaru estate next to where the graves are located.

In the past, around the Plateau area, four suspected treasure hunters were busted by members of the public, while three others managed to escape after they were found exhuming ancient graves.

The suspects had already dug open a grave, leaving exposed skeletal remains and had started exhuming a second one before they were busted by residents while in the act.

The bizarre afternoon incident saw the suspects beaten up by an angry mob after a woman who saw them and raised the alarm.

Recovered from the suspects' pockets were suspected assorted herbs believed to be witchcraft paraphernalia, business cards from funeral homes and several mobile phones.

They noted it was not their first time to dig from graves.

“We were searching for valuables we believe the dead had been buried with. The demand for relics and old ornaments have a lucrative market abroad,” one of them explained.

He added that their main target in exhumations was antique padlocks, iron boxes, gold and silver ornaments believed to be buried with their owners.

High demand for antiquities in foreign countries

The hunter explained that they had already made money from earlier sales of antiquities which he noted was high in demand abroad.

“This is not something that exists in our community; it is a taboo, the dead must never be disturbed. The graves are very well respected. Let us allow the dead to rest in peace,” said Wellington Korir, a resident.

Speaking to TUKO.co.ke, Centre Against Torture Director Kimutai Kirui urged the Uasin Gishu county government to surrender the cemetery to any institution wishing to preserve it.

“If the county government cannot preserve this historical site, then it should be surrendered to any institution, preferably a university, to preserve it for academic purposes and future generations,” said Kirui.

He challenged the regional government to come forward and explain to the locals what it is doing to preserve the facility.

County directors Haji Dakane Mohamed (culture) and Margaret Aiyabei (tourism) said that the two departments were closely working with the national government on identifying and preserving such historical sites.

“We have been working on a document that will enable us to identify all the heritage sites such as the Eldoret War Cemetery so that together with the national government we can preserve them because they are important to us,” Dakane told TUKO.co.ke in an interview.
